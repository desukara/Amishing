Who are the Amish?

The Amish are a group of traditional Christian communities known for simple living, strong family values, and a commitment to faith, humility, and separation from the modern world.

A simple explanation

The Amish are not a single organization or denomination with one central authority. Instead, they are made up of many local church communities, each led by its own leaders and following shared traditions.

They are part of a broader group known as Anabaptist Christians, which began in Europe during the 1500s. Their beliefs emphasize adult baptism, obedience to Scripture, humility, and living a life separate from worldly influences.

Today, most Amish communities are found in the United States, especially in Pennsylvania, Ohio, and Indiana.

Key things to understand

Faith comes first

Amish life is centered on Christianity, obedience, and living in a way that reflects humility and devotion to God.

Community over individualism

Decisions are made with the church and community in mind rather than personal preference.

Limited technology

Technology is not rejected completely, but carefully controlled to protect family, faith, and community life.

Deep historical roots

The Amish trace their origins back to European Christian reform movements in the 16th century.

Common misunderstandings

Many people think the Amish reject all technology, never interact with outsiders, or live exactly the same way everywhere. These ideas are often oversimplified or incorrect.

In reality, Amish communities differ from one another, and many decisions about daily life are made at the local level.

Understanding the Amish requires looking beyond stereotypes and taking time to learn how their beliefs shape their way of life.
